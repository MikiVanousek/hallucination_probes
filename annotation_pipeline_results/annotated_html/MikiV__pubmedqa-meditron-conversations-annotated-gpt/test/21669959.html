user_prompt: Secondhand smoke risk in infants discharged from an NICU: potential for significant health disparities?
yes ## 1 Secondhand smoke exposure (SHSe) is a serious health risk to children, especially infants and children with respiratory conditions. In the United States, the majority of children are exposed to secondhand smoke. Infants who are born at very low birth weight (<1500 g) are at increased risk for respiratory conditions, such as chronic lung disease, wheezing, and pneumonia. These conditions are exacerbated by SHSe. In addition, secondhand smoke exposure is associated with an increased risk of sudden infant death syndrome. It is estimated that 20% of all deaths due to sudden infant death syndrome are attributable to SHSe. SHSe also causes a range of health conditions, including asthma, bronchitis, and ear infections, in children. The purpose of this paper is to examine the relationship between socioeconomic status, race, and mental health status on secondhand smoke exposure and smoking bans in families with a high-risk infant in a neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) who report a smoker in the household. ## 2 This study was conducted as part of The Baby's Breath Project, a randomized controlled trial designed to reduce secondhand smoke exposure in families with a high-risk infant in the NICU who report a smoker in the household. The study was conducted at a large, urban, tertiary care hospital in Houston, Texas. Eligibility criteria included being a parent or legal guardian of an infant born at very low birth weight (<1500 g) who was currently in the NICU and was expected to be discharged home within the next 2 weeks. Families were also required to report a smoker in the household. The study was approved by the Institutional Review Board of the hospital, and all families provided informed consent to participate. ## 3 Data were collected on a total of 99 families who met the eligibility criteria. The majority of families (64.6%) were Hispanic, and 50.5% were from a low-income family (annual income < $25,000). The majority of families (84.8%) had a household smoker, and 46.2% of families reported having a total smoking ban in place in both their homes and cars. The average age of mothers was 27.8 years, and 68.7% of mothers were married.
